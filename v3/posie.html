The Heart of the Garden

Meet Posie

She arrived small and uncertain, and she changed everything.

The Beginning

She came from under the cottage.

We didn't go looking for a cat. We were tending the beds one afternoon — pulling weeds between the zinnias, staking the dahlias — when a small, uncertain shape appeared at the edge of the garden. She'd been living under the old cottage at the back of the lot, sheltering in the shadow of the foundation.

She was thin. Wary. But she stayed close — watching us work the way cats do, with a kind of calm assessment that said she already knew she was home, even if we hadn't caught up yet.

We called her Posie. After the small, meaningful bundles of flowers people once carried to say things words couldn't. It suited her perfectly.

The Hard Winter

She endured the storm.

The ice storm came in January. The kind that glazes everything — turns the garden into glass, snaps branches from trees that have stood for decades. We worried about the beds, about the bulbs, about the delicate things.

But Posie was fine. She had found her footing by then, made her rounds between the raised beds, curled into the sunlit patches of the porch. She was sturdier than she looked — as rescued things often are. All that time under the cottage had taught her something about endurance.

"By spring she was chasing bees through the zinnias, pressing her nose into the dahlias. The garden was hers. It always had been, really."

A Season of Slowness

The blooms were her sanctuary.

When Posie's health began to quietly decline, the garden became her sanctuary in a new way. She moved more slowly, chose her resting spots with more intention. She still came to watch us work — still found the warmest patch of afternoon light — but the world had narrowed down to the most beautiful parts of it.

We planted more zinnias that year. More dahlias. More of everything she loved to sniff and watch and sit beside. If a garden can be a kind of love language, ours was speaking directly to her.

Those months taught us something about what it means to tend something — not just plants, but living things. The way care and attention can become a form of grace.

Her Legacy

Her legacy blooms on.

Posie is gone now — but she is in everything here. She is in the name of every small bouquet we tie. She is in the way we tend the beds with extra care, the way we pause to notice what's blooming. She is in every download, every print, every wrapped posie handed across the flower cart.

Backyard Bloomstead exists because of the garden. And the garden, in so many ways, became what it is because of her.

"She taught us that a backyard can be sacred. That something small — a cat, a flower, a handmade gift — can carry enormous meaning. That's what we try to do here. Every single day."
